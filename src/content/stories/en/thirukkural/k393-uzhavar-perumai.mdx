Arjun was a schoolboy from the city of Chennai. During his summer holidays, he travelled to the village where his grandfather and grandmother lived. The moment he stepped off the bus, he looked around — green paddy fields, coconut trees swaying in the breeze, and the distant tinkling of cowbells.

"It's all very pretty. But working in the fields looks like such dirty work!" Arjun wrinkled his nose.

His grandfather smiled. He was a farmer — sun-darkened skin, strong hands, and a calm light in his eyes.

"Come to the fields with me tomorrow morning. Then we'll talk," he said.

The next day, before the sun had even risen, Grandfather woke Arjun. The two of them walked to the fields. The soil was still damp. Water flowed gently through the channels. Rows of green seedlings lay neatly in the mud.

"These tiny seedlings will become golden sheaves of rice in a few months," Grandfather explained. "First, you have to plough the land. Then you sow the seeds. You have to make sure the water is just right. You have to pull out the weeds. You watch the sky, wondering whether the rains will come. You worry whether the sun is too harsh. And finally, you harvest."

Arjun listened in amazement. "That much work goes into a single grain of rice?"

"Yes, my boy. Behind every mouthful we eat lies months of hard work," Grandfather said.

Arjun stepped into the field and tried to plant a seedling. The cool mud stuck to his feet. At first, he laughed. But within ten minutes, his back ached. His hands grew tired.

"Grandpa, can I take a break?" he panted.

Grandfather chuckled softly. "Of course. But farmers work like this for hours on end. They don't stop for the sun or the rain."

Arjun stood quietly, looking across the field. He thought of his school canteen. He thought of the plates of rice his mother served at home. Until now, he had always seen rice as just an ordinary thing. Now, in every single grain, he could see the farmer's sweat.

As they sat in the shade of a tree, Grandfather recited a couplet from the Thirukkural:

*"The might of farmers is the might of all;*
*without them, who else could truly live?"*

"What does it mean, Grandpa?" Arjun asked.

"The strength of the farmers is the strength of the whole world. Only because they toil can everyone else live," Grandfather said.

When Arjun returned to the city, he told his friends: "Farming is not dirty work. It is the noble work that feeds the entire world."

**Farmers feed the world — we must always honour their toil with gratitude.**
